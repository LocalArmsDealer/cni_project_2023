Introduction

Fossil fuels power most of the US. According to The Department of Energy, around 65% of energy production in 2021 was from fossil fuels, with the majority being from natural gas (graph below ↓↓↓). Of the remaining 35%, around 20% is nuclear power. Should that number be higher, is it high enough, or should it be lower? I don't have an answer, but I can provide information and a lot of sources.

Viewpoint 1: More Nuclear

Nuclear power produced 772 billion kWh (kilowatt hours) of power in 2022 according to the Energy Information Administration, which released a total of 0 p/kWh (pounds per kilowatt hour) of carbon. If that power were produced via natural gas, that would release 0.97 p/kWh (Source) for a total of approximately 374.42 million tons of carbon, all of which would be released into the atmosphere. Since excess carbon causes temperatures to rise, we want less of it in the atmosphere. Nuclear power doesn't release carbon, so nuclear power is a good alternative to fossil fuels.

Nuclear power plants generate around 500,000 high-paying jobs, along with contributing $60mil to the US GDP per year according to the DoE. Those jobs improve the US economy, along with generating power for cities in the US. Nuclear power produces 19% of energy in the United States, which is a low amount compared to fossil fuel usage. If we increased the amount of nuclear power production to 30%, we could replace 11% of US energy production from fossil fuels. This would double the amount of jobs created by nuclear power and contribute more to the GDP without contributing to global warming.

Viewpoint 2: We Have Enough

Nuclear power plants aren't free. Construction, while technically possible to do in your own backyard (here's how), costs money. Maintenance isn't cheap either, and staff need to be paid quite a bit. That money needs to be gained by corporations and individuals paying you their electricity bills, which means people need to choose to buy your power. Since natural gas is cheaper than nuclear, most corporations don't buy from nuclear plants, meaning it's harder to make more nuclear plants. So, might as well keep the amount the same.

Even if you aren't concerned with cost, the fuel for nuclear reactors isn't just used for power plants. The use of fissile materials for nuclear weapons is always a concern, especially when you're the one authorizing the trade of fissile materials to build power plants in a country. Of course, this isn't as much of a concern for domestic corporations, since the buildings for nuclear power and nuclear weapons are very different, but there's always the possibility that some shady group is building a nuclear bomb or two underground. Of course, this is more of an emotional concern than a factual one, but it's still entirely possible.
